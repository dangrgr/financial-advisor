# January 2026 — Family Financial Summary

_Written for a quick read. No spreadsheets required._

---

## The Big Picture

January was a solid month with a few bumps — nothing alarming, and nothing we weren't set up to handle. Our regular income covered almost everything, and the financial cushion we built from last year's bonus is doing exactly what it was designed to do: absorb life's surprises without stress.

We ended the month in a stable position with strong savings, a healthy investment account, and a clear view of what's coming next.

---

## Income

Our take-home pay in January was **$10,799** from two regular paychecks, plus a small unexpected $691 payment from work (a one-time thing — we're not counting on it repeating). We also earned $227 in savings interest, which quietly grows in the background.

---

## How We Spent

Our total regular spending came to about **$11,000** — just a hair over what came in. That $200 gap is small, and it came out of our bonus fund automatically. Think of that fund as a buffer we intentionally built for exactly this kind of month.

### Where we did well

We came in well under budget in several big areas. We spent less than planned on dining and going out, which is great. Shopping was also well under — partly because some purchases came back as refunds. Our utility and subscription bills ran lower than expected, which gave us meaningful breathing room overall.

### Where we went over

A few categories ran higher than planned, and these are worth keeping in mind going into February:

- **Medical** was the biggest surprise — we spent almost double the budget. This one is hard to control, but it's worth watching to see if it's a trend or a one-time cluster of appointments and copays.

- **Groceries** ran about 10% over. With the family and everything we have going on, this isn't shocking, but there may be room to be a little more intentional there.

- **Education** came in about 20% over budget. This was likely a combination of school fees and materials — worth a quick look at what's coming up in the next month or two.

- **The car** had a repair in January ($500 in the regular budget category, not the big one below). This pushed vehicle costs over plan for the month.

None of these are emergencies — they're just the areas where a little awareness in February can make a difference.

---

## The Car Repair — A Special Note

Separate from regular spending, the Subaru needed a significant repair in early January: **$4,746**. We've decided to cover this from our bonus fund rather than letting it disrupt our monthly budget. This is exactly the kind of unexpected expense the bonus fund is there for. It stings, but it's handled — and it doesn't change our monthly picture at all.

---

## Our Bonus Fund — The Safety Net

Last December, Dan received his annual work bonus of **$77,341**. Here's the simple version of where it went:

- **$50,000** went straight into savings to shore up our emergency fund. That money sits in Capital One earning interest and we don't touch it unless something serious happens.
- The remaining **$27,341** became our "bonus pool" — a working fund for the year to cover gaps, one-time expenses, and deliberate splurges.

After December spending and January's car repair, gap coverage, and a few discretionary purchases (the Girl Scout cookie orders and a final mattress payment), the confirmed pool balance stands at **$15,777**.

We also have one known upcoming expense from the pool: our **IRS tax payment** of approximately **$5,000** due in April. After that, the pool would sit at roughly **$10,777**.

The good news: we're not just relying on that $10,777. The savings account holds more than we technically need for the emergency fund floor, which means roughly **$39,000 in savings is also available** if we ever need it. Together, that's about **$49,000 in flexible reserves** — a genuinely comfortable cushion for the rest of 2026.

---

## The Longer View

Beyond day-to-day savings, we have a Vanguard investment account worth about **$147,000**. We've designated $40,000 of that as a deeper emergency backstop (invested in stable S&P 500 index funds), and the rest — about $107,000 — is our longer-term freedom and growth fund. We're leaving it invested.

On top of all of this, our combined retirement accounts (IRAs and 401(k)s) are sitting at approximately **$1,000,000**. That's not money we're counting in our day-to-day plan, but it's a meaningful foundation for our future.

---

## Looking Ahead

For February, the main asks are:

1. **Medical and health costs** — keep an eye on whether spending stays elevated or was a January blip.
2. **Groceries** — see if we can trim back toward plan. Even $100–150 less per month adds up.
3. **Education** — nothing to change necessarily, just worth knowing what's on the calendar.

The car situation, the IRS bill, and the monthly gap are all accounted for. We're not scrambling. We're planning — and that's exactly where we want to be.
