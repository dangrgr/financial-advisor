# Pre-Retirement Income Strategy (Gap Years 55–65)

**Companion to:** `retirement_projection.py` and the May 2026 context brief
**Status:** Synthesis v1 — decision framework, not a commitment
**Premise:** A side-hustle that turns “retire comfortably” into “vacation comfortably,” built *while the W-2 still pays for everything*, and shaped to respect the 55–65 bridge plumbing.

-----

## 1. What the plan actually allows

The retirement plan sets two boundaries that decide which side-hustles fit, and they point the same direction.

**Capital is tight by design.** The plan’s liquidity is committed. The taxable brokerage (~$707k at 2034) **is** the 55–59½ living-expense bridge — spending it on a business forces Rule of 55 / Roth basis / 72(t) to cover living costs instead. The realistic ceiling for a business is **~$100–150k** from cash and the incoming gift; anything ≥$250k means re-projecting the bridge and likely sliding the spend goal to the $12k floor or delaying the date. So a capital-heavy venture isn’t a tradeoff to weigh — it’s outside the envelope.

**Income is worth more than deployed capital anyway**, for two reasons:

1. **It offsets the bridge directly.** Every $1k/mo of profit reduces bridge withdrawals ~$12k/yr, and the model treats earned income as pure upside. A low-capital, income-producing hustle is *structurally* more plan-compatible than a capital-heavy one — not just preferable.
1. **Income in 2034–2039 is portfolio insurance.** Sequence-of-returns risk in the first five retirement years is the named #1 risk. A business throwing off income in exactly those years means selling fewer depressed shares in a bad market. That is the single most valuable property a side-hustle can have here.

**Net effect:** the right side-hustles are low-capital and income-producing — which is also the set that’s most *you*: security IP built on infrastructure you’ve already designed, and small software run on agents you’ve already built. Capital-heavy real assets (NNN, self-storage, car wash, RV/boat lots) are out, on capital alone.

## 2. Decision criteria (the gap-year lens)

Ranked priorities (from the interview): **1. Passive / low hours · 2. Intellectual fun · 3. Building equity to sell · 4. Max income.**

Hard constraints (from the gap-year framing):

- **Capital ≤ ~$150k**, sourced from cash/gift, never the bridge.
- **Low-risk, slow-and-steady.** Modest growth, low churn, no get-rich-quick.
- **Travel-proof.** You and Terri disappear for two weeks at a stretch; minimal phone/laptop touch; hired help as stop-gap.
- **Seasonal effort is fine.** Heavy build sprints followed by low-touch maintenance.
- **Personality fit.** Calm, deep, sells through *execution* not upfront pitching. Distribution must be execution-shaped (content, citations, PLG), not charisma-shaped.
- **Tax-aware.** Income stacks on the $190k/yr Roth conversion plan and on ACA MAGI — so the ideal business has a *throttle*.

## 4. The recommended portfolio

### Core — Training labs + newsletter, with Hermes as the shared engine

The single best fit on every axis that matters. Zero capital, builds 2026–2033 on the W-2’s dime, and matures into a mostly-automated asset throwing off income exactly when the bridge needs it. Seasonal-effort shaped, travel-survivable (content banks ahead; labs self-run), B2B-expensed low churn, and the income dial is adjustable year to year — which turns out to be a *tax* feature (see §5).

What it actually is, post-Hermes: not an aggregation newsletter competing with a hundred free digests, but **original analysis of confirmed events, surfaced and contextualized before the crowd caught the significance.** Hermes is a discovery aid for you — it gets you oriented earlier and points you at primary sources faster. The published product still rides on confirmable, citable reporting.

> **Locked constraint:** Hermes is never a publish-first engine. Nothing names a victim org or individual on the strength of model correlation. The bar is public confirmation or a primary source you can point to. This is a floor, not a tunable threshold. No liability risk is acceptable if it means defaming an org or individual.

The training labs (“hands-on agentic attack/defense — the HackTheBox lane the cert bodies can’t easily build”) and the newsletter share one audience: findings become lab scenarios, the newsletter is how the audience finds you without you ever selling. The generic “AI security 101 course” lane is already lost (CompTIA SecAI+ launched Feb 2026; OSAI/CAISP established) — the defensible niche is *labs* (an infrastructure problem, which is your craft) and *corporate team training* (a B2B sale driven by board mandates, where 54% of orgs have AI policies but only 38% provide training).

### Co-core — One micro-SaaS acquisition

The play is **one boring, sticky B2B product** (~$2.5–4k MRR, ~4x earnings), run on your agentic maintenance rails. Funded from cash (~$134k post-gift) plus a modest seller note — never the brokerage. Multiples favor the buyer (marketplace medians fell from ~17x in 2022 to ~3.9x by end-2025 at ~70% margins), and the distress is almost always founder-burnout / key-man risk / maintenance debt — exactly the risk your agent fleet neutralizes and other buyers can’t.

This is the **equity-to-sell** leg (priority #3): buy-improve-sell with clear multiple expansion, or just hold it for cash flow. Discipline: *one* acquisition as a learning rep, gated hard (see §6), evaluate honestly before any second.

### Reserve — vCISO platform and cherry-picked consulting

Not part of the base plan; levers to pull if wanted.

- **vCISO platform** only in its execution-compatible forms: channel-delivered (sell to a handful of MSPs who white-label your capability — a technical demo to a technical buyer, not a pitch to a CFO) or platform-build (which converges with the micro-SaaS “build instead of buy” fork). Avoid the version where you’re the charming face selling retainers.
- **Consulting** is the emergency-income dial: highest $/hr, instant, infinitely throttleable — but it sells *hours*, builds *no equity*, and stops when you stop. Keep it in reserve for a bad-market year where you want income without drawing down. (Also: check the conflict-of-interest line with Locally Compact before anything client-facing.)

## 5. How a side-hustle interacts with the plan (the crown jewel)

This is the part no generic advice covers, and it’s where the *throttle* on these businesses becomes a planning instrument. **Directional logic below — model in the projection script and confirm with the CPA before acting.**

### 5.1 The bridge (the payoff)

Income in **2034–2039** reduces draws from the ~$707k brokerage at ~$12k/yr per $1k/mo of profit. At a $3–6k/mo target, that’s **$36–72k/yr less drawn** from the pool the model exhausts around age 60 — materially extending it and de-risking the stress case, while simultaneously hedging the #1 sequence-of-returns risk. This is the best feature of the whole strategy, and it’s why income-now beats capital-locked-up.

### 5.2 The Roth conversion tension (and its elegant resolution)

The plan converts **~$190k/yr (2034–2043)** filling to the top of the 22% bracket (~$207k MFJ taxable). Side income **stacks on top** of conversions — so naively it either (a) pushes conversions into 24–32% territory, or (b) forces smaller conversions, leaving more pre-tax money to become the RMD/widow’s-penalty problem at 75.

Two tools resolve this, and they’re why the *throttle* matters:

- **Conversion sizing as the release valve.** In high-side-income years, convert less; in lean years, convert more. The business’s adjustable income and the conversion plan are two halves of one annual tax decision.
- **The solo 401(k) as the absorber.** Self-employment income opens a solo 401(k). Critically, the *timing lines up*: pre-2034 the employee-deferral limit is consumed by your day-job 401(k), so only employer-side room exists — but **post-2034 (the gap years) the full solo 401(k) space opens**, exactly when you need to shelter side income to preserve 22%-bracket conversion headroom. The solo 401(k) lets side-hustle dollars *not* count against the conversion plan. This is the single most important interaction to model.

### 5.3 ACA / MAGI (probably already spent)

Pre-65 health insurance is budgeted at $15k/person/yr unsubsidized (watchlist: $18–24k by 2034). Side income raises MAGI — **but the $190k/yr conversion plan very likely already pushes MAGI past ACA subsidy eligibility**, which means the *incremental* MAGI impact of side income on ACA may be near-moot pre-65. **Confirm this** — if true, it removes one of the two objections to side income entirely. Conversely, a business with a group health option, or the self-employed health insurance deduction, could turn the ACA line from a cost into a partial benefit.

### 5.4 The cost side (don’t ignore it)

- **SE tax** ~15.3% on net SE earnings (the Medicare portion uncapped), plus **CO ~4.4%** state. Net-of-tax income is meaningfully below gross — but still pure upside to a plan that counts none of it.
- **Entity structure:** an **S-corp election** becomes worth the payroll-admin overhead once net income clears roughly $50–80k (splits salary vs. distribution to save SE tax on the distribution portion). You’ve already looked at CO LLC formation for Pinchy — same machinery. Light touch until income is real.
- **SS record:** SE income keeps earning years on the record. Marginal benefit (you’re near max already), but non-zero, especially if it replaces any low-earning years in the AIME calc.

## 7. Risks and off-ramps

- **The build sprint is real** (200+ hours of lab/content work before meaningful revenue). Mitigated by it being your hobby and day job already, and by the W-2 carrying all of life during the build.
- **Key-person taste risk** on the newsletter — softened, not eliminated, by Hermes making the *system* (not just your judgment) the asset.
- **Cert-body encroachment** — if CompTIA/OffSec ship a quality agentic-lab track in 2027, the individual-learner market compresses. **Corporate team training survives that scenario**, so weight toward B2B.
- **Micro-SaaS platform/API dependency** — a product built on one upstream API can die overnight. Diligence this hard; it’s a threat-modeling exercise, which is your edge.
- **Original-analysis liability** — fully addressed by the §4 locked constraint. Publish from what’s publicly confirmable, never from what’s merely reachable.
- **Off-ramp:** every piece is independently abandonable. Newsletter can go dormant, labs can stop refreshing, the micro-SaaS can be re-sold. Nothing here is a one-way door except the acquisition, which is gated.
